My Thinking Style

15 de mayo 2015

I took this short online quiz to reveal my thinking style and was not surprised with the result.

As an educator and coach to new teachers, I'm surprised and frustrated that I haven't come across this study and quiz before. Why? This information is powerful and meaningful for teachers - as learners - and teachers - as teachers.

So often I worked to reveal how my students learn best but I never considered that their way of thinking is not the same as their way of learning.

If I step back out of my role as teacher and put on my student hat I am very excited about the clarity I've gained in how I think and that it isn't the same as how I learn. I feel empowered and awakened!

However, I am hesitant to wear a name-tag that says: "Hello, I'm a Concrete-Random Thinker" and wear the label. You know how you have to check a box for race or gender or occupation? I get that strange gut feeling when I am asked to check those boxes. Yes, I identify as a white female and my occupation box: education is usually on there. But when you unpack that classification "white" that means I'm not....x, y or z. Yup, that's a conversation for another day.ha, what a prime example of a random thinker...

But when I think about my thinking-style: concrete-random and its signature thinking traits like:

  • enjoy experimentation and discovery
  • concrete: use your 5 senses to understand the present, don't look for hidden meanings or imaginative ideas or feelings
  • organize information in chunks, and in no particular order. No need to follow all the steps or even follow them in 'order.'

I searched for more information about the concrete-random thinking style and found this amazing resource (below). I can check off almost everything on this list!

Thinking Style != Learning Style

I love to learn. Now I can add that I am fascinated by the science of learning - how do we learn best? what strategies help me learn? I truly and deeply believe that learning leads to growth and to grow as a sister/educator/global citizen, I need to continously learn and develop. One of my values in life is to live with a sense of possibility. Believe that your vision is possible.

But it isn't easy. I have struggled to learn. I get stuck and feel like my head is jammed into muck and darkness. When I first tried to write some methods from Learn Ruby the Hard Way I typed character by character without seeing how it worked or why something didn't work. I'd feel frustrated that I wasn't 'seeing it' but I didn't want to stop trying. I wanted to figure out how to solve a coding challenge but recognized my low skill level and ugh, that just isn't fun.

There are times where I get so focused I can't see any other way of solving a problem. I'm learning about that: the diffused mode and focused mode in our brains. Now I know that I perceive new ideas and skills with a concrete perception: I need to see/feel/hear what is going on to develop an understanding. I'm not so good at starting with the abstract and imaginative way of thinking about something new. Makes sense why I learned Spanish while living in Santiago - I could hear it everyday! Back to learning code...

I like learning to code because it is experimental. I can try something and see what I built right away. This past week was our intro to CSS and my experience learning CSS positioning is a perfect example of how my thinking style affected my learning. I struggled with positioning my elements on my website and that is because I did not read a lot about CSS. I skipped some details and tried things on my own before reading the how-tos but still felt a gap in what I was doing with what I should understand. I knew I wasn't learning the positioning properties at a rate that would get me to a confident and comfortable place.

How did I overcome that struggle?

Collaboration! Imagine that - learning with others is more powerful and meaningful than learning alone. I have some fabulous Grasshoppers and through a pairing session and a chat with a new friend I saw the bigger picture. Plus, I went to yoga, slept and came back to it the next day. Sometimes, sleep is the best strategy.
